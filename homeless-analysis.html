Federal Spending on Homelessness

On a single night in 2016, 549,928 people experienced homelessness in the United States.

Multiple federal, state, and local programs offer support to people experiencing homelessness. Our analysis uses federal financial data to show the breakdown of federal funding spent to address homelessness.

How individuals experiencing homelessness are counted

Determining how many people are experiencing homelessness at a given time can be challenging. People experiencing homelessness are counted once a year (in late January) by grantees who receive funding from HUD’s Continuum of Care program. This program — the largest federal program related to homelessness — requires grantees to collect reliable data on the total number of people experiencing homelessness who reside in the grantee’s geographic area. These data are widely considered to be a low-end estimate, given the multiple challenges in counting individuals and families not identified with a specific location.

The geographic areas that homeless people are counted within are called Continuum of Care regions, and they reflect how each community organizes itself and applies for funding from HUD’s Continuum of Care program. HUD encourages Continuum of Care grantees to use multiple approaches to complete their count, if necessary. Grantees may employ a census approach, sample, or combine the methods.

Federal programs that address homelessness

The Department of Housing and Urban Development plays a lead role in federal efforts to address homelessness, though multiple federal agencies manage programs that provide supporting services including education, employment, housing resources, and more.

Our analysis identified 33 federal programs that explicitly flagged homeless individuals as beneficiaries. These included programs that named homeless individuals as primary targets of services, or as one of the program’s target recipient groups. We found that programs targeting homelessness focus on either all individuals experiencing homelessness; or on a subset, such as veterans or youth. These programs provide various types of assistance, which we divided into six categories: housing, food, education, employment, health, and support services.

Federal funding is just one piece of the puzzle — states, municipalities, and other organizations play a large role in providing funding to address homelessness. While we did not conduct a comprehensive analysis of the relationship between federal, state, local, and private funding sources for homelessness-alleviation programs across the country, numerous examples illustrate that federal funding works as part of a larger network of support. For example, the city of New York spent $1.6 billion on homelessness programs in 2017, compared with about $250 million from federal programs (including those where homeless individuals are one of several beneficiaries). The city of San Francisco spent $275 million on similar programs in 2017, compared with the $63 million they received in federal funding. And, to cite an example of private funding, the Church of Latter Day Saints has given $42 million (over the last ten years) to build housing for chronically homeless individuals in Salt Lake City.

How we conducted this analysis

We conducted this analysis using data from the following sources:

  • Federal grant award data from USAspending.gov
  • Federal program descriptions from the Catalogue of Federal Domestic Assistance (CFDA)
  • Counts of people experiencing homelessness from the Department of Housing and Urban Development’s (HUD) 2017 Point-in-Time count

To determine the amount the federal government spends addressing homelessness, we reviewed federal program descriptions using the CFDA, looking for keywords related to homelessness. We then developed a ranking system to assess whether individuals experiencing homelessness would directly benefit from these programs, keeping only the most relevant programs in our dataset. We also categorized these programs by the type of service they provide. For example, the Homeless Veterans Reintegration Program provides services to homeless veterans seeking meaningful employment, which we classified as employment-related. We presented these programs to the US Interagency Council on Homelessness, which provided feedback on our ranking system.

We used this dataset to identify all grant spending from USAspending.gov related to the programs we determined have a direct relation to homelessness programs. As part of our analysis, we identified two categories of grant awards in USAspending.gov data that are relevant to our work: those that were awarded to a Continuum of Care area (an administrative region recognized by HUD) or fell within its geographic area, and those that didn’t have specific location data or that applied to multiple areas within a state. To create our map visualization, we mapped funding amounts for the programs that fell within Continuum of Care areas using shape files provided by HUD. The data used in this analysis were updated as of January 2018.
